Strategy Layer
North Star Framework
A North Star metric is a single leading indicator that connects revenue increase with trackable user behavior that proves the customer is getting the core value from the product.

Momentus delivers value across three main lifecycle product areas, so we define one end-to-end metric that proves the platform is working as a whole, and one product area-level metric per stage that shows each link in the chain is carrying its weight. If any product area isn't aligned to its North Star, the end-to-end metric drops — making alignment both a strategic compass and an early warning system.

Momentus Events (ME)
The number of events that progress through at least three lifecycle stages — intake, planning, and execution — entirely within the Momentus platform during a rolling 30-day window, across all active accounts.
Not a vanity metric Expresses customer value Leading indicator of revenue

Effectiveness · Task success
% of events completing all three ME stages
Percentage of events that successfully move through all three stages (booking, planning, execution) inside Momentus within the rolling 30-day window. The North Star itself, as a rate.
Why ME — rationale
Why not revenue or bookings?
Revenue is a lagging indicator. Bookings only measure Alex's success. ME measures whether the entire promise — book it, plan it, execute it — is delivered inside Momentus. It's the only metric that captures all three products working together.
What does ME tell us that ARR doesn't?
ARR tells you what customers paid. ME tells you whether they got what they paid for. An account that pays for all three products but executes operations on paper hasn't realized the platform's value — and is at high churn risk.
How does ME align PM, Engineering, and Design?
Any feature that doesn't move ME is a distraction. Any friction that breaks the MB → MP → MJ chain is a product failure. ME gives every team a shared definition of what "done" means for a venue.
◆ North Star · Sales Product Area
Momentus Bookings (MB)
The number of event bookings that are created, contracted, and signed entirely inside Momentus during a rolling 30-day window — without the process falling back to a separate CRM, email thread, or paper contract. A booking counts only when the entire intake-to-contract workflow completes inside the platform.

Percentage of active sales users in a given month who were also active the prior month for booking creation. Consistent return signals Momentus has replaced external CRMs and email as the primary booking tool.
Efficiency · Time to value

Median hours from first lead record creation to a booking status of "Definite" inside Momentus. A decreasing cycle time signals friction reduction in the qualification-to-close workflow.
Effectiveness · Task success
% bookings with signed contract in-platform
Percentage of confirmed bookings where the contract was generated, sent, and signed entirely inside Momentus — without fallback to Word, DocuSign, or email attachment. The direct measure of whether MB counts.
Why MB — grounded in the research
The core failure MB addresses
The vertical research found that many venues use Momentus to track bookings but complete the actual contracting in email, Word, or separate CRMs. MB only counts when the full intake-to-contract workflow completes inside the platform — it cannot be gamed by logging a booking that was really done elsewhere.
Why not total bookings created?
Total bookings is gameable: a sales rep can log a booking after the fact, or log it inside Momentus while doing the real work in Salesforce. MB requires the process — lead qualification, availability check, proposal, contract — to happen inside Momentus. That's the signal that the platform replaced the external tool.
How MB starts the ME chain
MB is the handoff trigger. When Alex closes a booking in-platform, Sam has something to pick up inside Momentus. If Alex logs the booking after closing it in a CRM, the handoff is broken — and ME cannot start cleanly. MB measures whether Alex gave Sam a clean baton.

Why MP — grounded in the research
The core failure MP addresses
The vertical research explicitly states: "Elite customers tend to detail and share event needs in Word/Excel. Downstream this makes ops more manual." MP only counts when Sam documents and distributes the full plan inside Momentus — triggering a clean handoff to Jordan.
Why not events created or functions added?
Creating a function record doesn't mean the plan is complete or shared. MP requires both completion (all detail fields populated) and distribution (shared to ops inside the platform). A half-finished BEO sent via email is a failure state for ME.
How MP connects to ME
MP is the middle link. If Sam's detail lives in Word, Jordan has nothing to execute from inside Momentus, and ME cannot complete. Every MP event is an event Jordan can pick up in-platform — the bridge between sales and operations.
◆ North Star · Operations Product Area
Momentus Jobs (MJ)
The number of events where all assigned tasks — setup, execution, teardown, incident response — are created, tracked, and closed inside Momentus during the event execution window (pre-event, day-of, post-event) in a rolling 30-day window. An event counts only when task execution does not fall back to paper, radio, or email.
Completes the ME chain Closes the paper/radio execution gap Direct measure of operational value realization

Why MJ — grounded in the research
The core failure MJ addresses
The vertical research is explicit: "AMER customers use more paper-based manual process for job assignment and communication." Jordan's execution currently happens on printed sheets, radio, and email follow-up. MJ rises only when that execution moves inside the platform — it cannot be gamed by creating tasks and never updating them.
Why MJ is the hardest and most valuable metric
The value realization gap is largest in operations. Enterprise customers range "from using customized reports to MWO to no ops in Momentus due to usability, cost, and learning curve." MJ directly measures whether that gap is closing — and it's the gap the convention center research identified as most underserved.
How MJ completes ME
MJ is the final link. MB hands off a contracted booking. MP hands off a detailed plan. MJ completes the loop by executing that plan inside the platform. An event that passes MB → MP → MJ is a ME event. Jordan is the last mile.

Human Layer
Ontological Persona Definition
An ontological persona definition is not a demographic profile — it is a philosophical breakdown of who a person fundamentally is: the fixed conditions that make up their identity, the dynamic states they move through, and the structural status or role they occupy within an organization.

Momentus serves three distinct people across the event lifecycle — a sales manager, an event coordinator, and an operations supervisor. Each one brings a different relationship to the event, a different definition of success, and a different set of jobs to be done. Understanding who they are at this depth is what allows us to design for their reality, not just their workflow — making Momentus feel like it was built for them, not just used by them.

Semantic Layer
Semantic Interaction Design + Ontological Object Definition
Semantic interaction design is the practice of grounding a product's interface in the real-world concepts of its domain — defining the primary objects, the actions that can be performed on them, and the attributes that describe their state, before a single screen is designed.

Momentus operates across sales, planning, and operations — three product areas that share a common subject: the event. Establishing a shared conceptual grammar across these areas ensures that language, structure, and behavior are consistent, reducing cognitive load and integration friction for every persona. Without this foundation, each product area risks developing its own vocabulary and logic — creating silos that contradict each other and make the platform harder to learn, harder to use, and harder to build.

Innovation Layer
Jobs to Be Done Framework
A job to be done is the underlying goal a person is trying to achieve in a given situation — not the feature they use, but the outcome they need. It is defined by the functional result they're after, the emotional state they want to reach, and the social signal they want to send.

Mapping the jobs of Momentus users reveals where the platform creates real value, where it falls short, and where the most meaningful innovation opportunities lie. Each job has phases, desired outcomes, and metrics that make it measurable — turning user needs into a structured foundation for product decisions. Features built without a job to be done are guesses. Features built against a validated job are investments — and this layer is what separates one from the other.
